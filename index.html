The Race to the North Pole

The Explorers

The second half of the 1800s has often been called the time of the heroic polar explorers. The inhospitable and dangerous regions around the North and South Poles emerged as challenges to male courage and technological achievements. Andrée's plans for his balloon trip fit well into the pattern of the time.

The Departure

Andrée planned to depart in 1896, and had many willing candidates for the few places as expedition participants. He chose the brilliant student Nils Strindberg, who did research in physics and chemistry, and the 27-year-old engineer Knut Frænkel, who was a civil engineer, athlete and mountain hiker as a meteorological observer.

The Expedition

Since Andrée, Strindberg and Frænkel took off with their balloon from Svalbard in July 1897, the balloon quickly lost too much hydrogen and was forced to land on the pack ice after only two days. The travelers were not injured on landing, but had a difficult journey back on foot over the ice.

The Mystery

When the Arctic winter came in October, the group camped on Vitön in Svalbard, where they died.


For 33 years, the fate of the expedition was one of the Arctic's unsolved mysteries, until the camp was discovered by chance in 1930. The discovery created a great sensation in Sweden, where the dead men had been regarded as lost heroes.
